M1: Introduction to Coaching

Learning Outcomes

  • Identify the multiple roles a coach must perform.
  • Understand different coaching styles and their impact.
  • Define and begin to formulate a personal coaching philosophy.
  • Recognize the three phases of skill acquisition for beginners.

Key Concepts

  • The Coach's Roles: A coach is a teacher, motivator, disciplinarian, organizer, public relations officer, fund raiser, advisor, friend, scientist, and student.
  • Coaching Styles: Styles range from ‘Authoritarian’ (strict, command‑style) to ‘Nice‑Guy’ (co‑operative). A successful coach often blends these styles.
  • Coaching Philosophy: What a coach genuinely believes is important for success; the foundation for actions and decisions. It includes a General Philosophy (values, relationships) and a Technical Philosophy (system of play).
  • Phases of Learning: Beginners learn in three phases: the Try it phase (conscious thought), the Mastering phase (associative), and the Automatic phase (autonomous).

Practical Drills

  • Coaching Style Role‑Play: Coach acts out a style while explaining a drill; players identify the style from communication cues.
  • Personal Philosophy Builder: Guided prompts to write a personal coaching philosophy—purpose, definition of success, and player treatment.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: According to the manual, what is a primary role of a coach?
Answer: A teacher, motivator, and disciplinarian.
Question: What are the three phases of learning a physical skill for a beginner coach to know?
Answer: Try it, mastering, automatic.
Question: A coach's philosophy is primarily about what?
Answer: What the coach genuinely believes is important to coaching success.
Question: Which coaching style is described as ‘strict, disciplined, punishes frequently’?
Answer: ‘Authoritarian’ Coach.
Question: The acronym COACH stands for Comprehensive, Outlook, Affection, Character, and...?
Answer: Humor.

M2: Rules & Player Positions

Learning Outcomes

  • State the number of players on the court and on a team.
  • Identify the 5 primary basketball positions.
  • Describe the main offensive and defensive responsibilities for each position.
  • Understand the basic scoring values.

Key Concepts

  • Number of Players: A team has 12 players, but only 5 are on the court at a time.
  • Positions: Roles are usually determined by height.
  • Position Roles:
    • Point Guard (PG/#1): The “coach on the floor” and primary ball‑handler.
    • Off‑Guard (SG/#2): Generally the best shooter.
    • Small Forward (SF/#3): The most versatile player.
    • Power Forward (PF/#4): Strong rebounder and interior player.
    • Center (C/#5): The tallest player, who protects the basket and scores inside.
  • Scoring: 3 points from outside the three‑point line, 2 points from inside, and 1 point for a free throw.

Practical Drills

  • Position Hotspots: Players sprint to cones at each position on the coach’s call to build court awareness.
  • Role Responsibilities Shout‑Out: Players rapidly name responsibilities for a called position—offense or defense.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: How many players per team are on the court during a regulation game?
Answer: 5.
Question: What is the primary offensive responsibility of a Point Guard?
Answer: Dribbling, passing, and setting up plays.
Question: A successful shot from outside the three‑point line is worth how many points?
Answer: 3.
Question: Which position is generally the tallest player on the team?
Answer: Center.
Question: Which player is also referred to as the #2 guard?
Answer: Off‑Guard.

M3: Violations

Learning Outcomes

  • Define what a violation is in basketball.
  • Differentiate between key violations like Traveling, Double Dribble, and Carrying.
  • Understand time‑based restrictions like the 3‑second, 5‑second, and 8‑second rules.
  • Know the penalty for a violation.

Key Concepts

  • Violation Definition: An infraction of the rules that does not involve illegal contact. The penalty is a throw‑in for the opposing team.
  • Ball Handling Violations:
    • Traveling: Illegally moving the pivot foot.
    • Double Dribble: Dribbling, stopping, and dribbling again.
    • Carrying/Palming: Hand goes under the ball while dribbling.
  • Time Restrictions:
    • 5‑Second Rule: To pass the ball inbounds.
    • 8‑Second Rule: To advance the ball from backcourt to frontcourt.
    • 3‑Second Rule: Offensive players cannot remain in the key for more than three seconds.
  • Other Violations:
    • Goaltending: Interfering with a shot on its downward flight.
    • Backcourt Violation: Illegally returning the ball to the backcourt.

Practical Drills

  • Pivot Perfection: After a jump stop, practice proper pivoting with a firmly planted pivot foot.
  • Dribble, Stop, Pass: Legal stop → pivot → pass to avoid traveling or double dribble.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: What is a ‘double dribble’ violation?
Answer: Both A and C (Dribbling with two hands simultaneously and Dribbling, stopping, then dribbling again).
Question: A ‘traveling’ violation occurs when a player moves their pivot foot illegally without doing what?
Answer: All of the above (Shooting, Passing, Dribbling).
Question: What is a ‘backcourt violation’?
Answer: Both A and B (Taking too long to cross half court; illegally returning the ball to backcourt).
Question: A player carrying the ball inbounds has how many seconds to pass the ball?
Answer: 5 seconds.
Question: ‘Carrying’ or ‘palming’ is a violation where a player's hand…
Answer: …goes under the ball, causing it to pause.

M4: Fouls & Official Signals

Learning Outcomes

  • Distinguish between a foul and a violation.
  • Identify different types of personal fouls (e.g., blocking, charging).
  • Understand the purpose of a technical foul.
  • Learn the correct 3‑step procedure for reporting a foul and recognize key official signals.

Key Concepts

  • Foul Definition: An infraction involving illegal physical contact with an opponent.
  • Types of Fouls:
    • Personal Foul: Illegal contact like hitting, pushing, or holding.
    • Charging: Offensive foul where the ball handler runs into a stationary defender.
    • Blocking: Defensive foul for impeding progress without position.
    • Technical Foul: Non‑contact, typically unsportsmanlike behavior.
  • Foul Reporting: 3‑step sequence — Number of Player → Type of Foul → Free Throws (or direction).
  • Key Signals: Hands on hips for blocking; rolling fists for traveling, etc.

Practical Drills

  • Referee Signal Mirror: Coach performs signals; players mirror to learn the game’s visual language.
  • Charge vs. Block Challenge: Controlled 1‑on‑1 to practice and differentiate charge vs. block.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: What is the main difference between a foul and a violation?
Answer: Fouls involve illegal physical contact; violations do not.
Question: What is the correct 3‑step sequence for reporting a foul?
Answer: Number of Player → Type of Foul → Free Throws/Direction.
Question: What is the official signal for a ‘blocking’ foul?
Answer: Both hands on hips.
Question: A ‘technical foul’ can be called for what reason?
Answer: Disrespectful or unsportsmanlike conduct.
Question: The signal for ‘traveling’ is:
Answer: Rotate fists.

M5: Equipment & Court

Learning Outcomes

  • State the standard dimensions of a basketball court.
  • Identify the regulation height of the hoop for different age groups.
  • Distinguish between the three main basketball sizes and their uses.
  • Recognize the key lines and areas of the court.

Key Concepts

  • Court Dimensions: Standard court size is 28m × 15m.
  • Hoop Height: 3.05 m (10 ft) for ages 12+; 2.60 m for under‑12.
  • Ball Sizes:
    • Size 7: Men’s competition.
    • Size 6: Women’s competition.
    • Size 5: Under‑12 (mini) basketball.
  • Court Markings: Sidelines, baselines, center circle, free‑throw line, and the “paint/key”.

Practical Drills

  • Court Landmark Tour: Players sprint and touch called lines/areas to lock in court geography.
  • Ball ID Line‑up: Identify the correct ball size by age/gender category.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: What is the standard court size for players aged 12 and above?
Answer: 28m × 15m.
Question: What is the regulation hoop height for ages 12 and above?
Answer: 3.05 m.
Question: Which ball size is used for Men’s competition?
Answer: Size 7.
Question: A Size 6 basketball is standard for which category?
Answer: Women.
Question: What is the standard jersey numbering range for players?
Answer: 4–15.

M6: Basic Skills — Movement & Ball Handling

Learning Outcomes

  • Understand the importance of balance as the foundation of all movement.
  • Differentiate between a ‘one count stop’ (jump stop) and a ‘two count stop’ (stride stop).
  • Define ‘pivoting’ and identify the pivot foot.
  • Recognize the ‘triple threat’ position and its three options.

Key Concepts

  • Balance: Good body position; knees flexed; weight on the balls of the feet.
  • Stops:
    • One Count Stop (Jump Stop): Land on both feet simultaneously.
    • Two Count Stop (Stride Stop): Land on one foot, then the other.
  • Pivoting: Keep one foot planted (pivot foot) while stepping with the other.
  • Triple Threat: Be ready to Shoot, Dribble, or Pass.

Practical Drills

  • Figure Eight Relay: Ball handling through figure‑eight patterns around legs for coordination.
  • Two‑Count Stop and Pivot: Stride stop → pivot → pass to reinforce legal footwork.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: What is the ‘one count stop’?
Answer: Landing on both feet at the same time.
Question: In pivoting, the foot that remains in contact with the floor is called the…
Answer: Pivot foot.
Question: What is key to good balance when moving on the court?
Answer: Head up, not leaning forward.
Question: The ‘two count stop’ involves…
Answer: Landing on one foot, followed by the other.
Question: The main purpose of the ‘triple threat’ position is to…
Answer: Be ready to shoot, pass, or dribble.

M7: Dribbling Skills

Learning Outcomes

  • Understand the correct biomechanics of dribbling.
  • Identify different types of dribbles for various situations (low, speed, change of pace).
  • Recognize key dribble moves like the crossover and hesitation.
  • Appreciate the importance of dribbling with your head up.

Key Concepts

  • Dribbling Biomechanics: Use fingertips and a wrist snap; keep head up to see the court.
  • Types of Dribbles:
    • Low Dribble: When closely guarded to protect the ball.
    • Speed Dribble: Move quickly in open court.
    • Change of Pace: Slow then accelerate to deceive a defender.
  • Dribble Moves:
    • Crossover: Push the ball hand‑to‑hand in front of the body.
    • Hesitation: Brief pause to freeze the defender.

Practical Drills

  • Cone Weave Challenge: Weave through cones using right, left, and crossover dribbles.
  • Pressure Dribble: Dribble inside a small square against pressure—protect the ball, eyes up.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: What is the primary purpose of the ‘low dribble’?
Answer: To be used when a player is closely guarded.
Question: The biomechanics of dribbling involve pushing the ball with the…
Answer: Fingers and a snap of the wrist.
Question: What is the key to a ‘change of pace’ dribble?
Answer: Slow down, then explode.
Question: The ‘crossover’ involves pushing the ball from one hand to the other…
Answer: In front of the body.
Question: When speed dribbling, a player should push the ball…
Answer: Ahead several feet.

M8: Shooting & Rebounding

Learning Outcomes

  • Identify the key components of shooting biomechanics.
  • Describe technique for lay‑up and jump shot.
  • Understand ‘boxing out’ for rebounding.
  • Differentiate offensive vs. defensive rebounding.

Key Concepts

  • Shooting Biomechanics: Body Balance, Aiming, Shooting Action (lift, elbow in), and strong Follow‑Through.
  • Types of Shots: Lay‑up, Jump Shot, Set Shot.
  • Rebounding: Gaining possession after a missed shot.
  • Boxing Out: Positioning between opponent and basket to secure rebound.

Practical Drills

  • Mikan Drill: Alternate lay‑ups with both hands under the basket.
  • Box Out War: 1‑on‑1 positioning and legal box‑out to secure rebounds.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: What are the biomechanics of shooting?
Answer: Body balance, aiming, shooting‑action, and follow‑through.
Question: For a lay‑up, aim for a spot…
Answer: On the board to bank the shot in.
Question: Most important element in defensive rebounding?
Answer: Position, timing, and securing the ball.
Question: A ‘hook shot’ is generally used by…
Answer: A tall player near the basket.
Question: Release angle for shooting is about…
Answer: 45 degrees.

M9: Strategy & Tactics

Learning Outcomes

  • Understand basic offensive plays (give‑and‑go, screen).
  • Describe objectives of a fast break.
  • Compare man‑to‑man vs. zone defense.
  • Recognize principles of transition defense.

Key Concepts

  • Basic Offensive Plays:
    • Give‑and‑Go: Pass and immediately cut to the basket for return pass.
    • Screen (‘Pick’): Stationary block on a defender to free a teammate.
  • Fast Break: Push the ball quickly to score before the defense sets.
  • Types of Defense: Man‑to‑Man (guard a player) vs. Zone (guard an area).
  • Transition Defense: Sprint back to prevent easy scores as first priority.

Practical Drills

  • Give and Go (2v0): Timing and execution without a defender.
  • Defend the Down Screen (3v0): Timing/spacing running off a screen.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: What is the ‘give‑and‑go’?
Answer: Pass to a teammate and cut to the basket for a return pass.
Question: A key principle of ‘Tandem Defense’?
Answer: Defenders remain constantly active and vocal.
Question: First objective of a fast break?
Answer: Create an easy, high‑percentage shot (e.g., lay‑up).
Question: A ‘screen’ in basketball is when an offensive player…
Answer: Becomes a stationary barrier to free a teammate.
Question: Three types of screens mentioned?
Answer: Up, Down, Cross; Single, Double, Staggered (all listed).

M10: Coaching Communication & Ethics

Learning Outcomes

  • Differentiate between verbal and non‑verbal communication.
  • Learn techniques for instructions and correcting errors.
  • Understand key principles of the Coaches’ Code of Conduct.
  • Appreciate the importance of being a positive role model.

Key Concepts

  • Communication Skills: Verbal (clear instructions) and Non‑verbal (body language, expressions).
  • Providing Feedback: Use the ‘sandwich’ method: positive → correction → positive.
  • Coaches’ Code of Conduct: Emphasizes fair play, respect, safety, and athlete well‑being. First point: treat each athlete as an individual.
  • Role of the Coach: Be a positive role model; lead by example.

Practical Drills

  • 30‑Second Demo: Explain and demonstrate a simple skill within 30 seconds.
  • Feedback Sandwich Practice: Practice delivering feedback using the sandwich model.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: Which of these is a form of non‑verbal communication?
Answer: Facial Expressions and Body Language.
Question: What is the first point in the Coaches’ Code of Conduct?
Answer: Treat each athlete as an individual.
Question: When correcting errors, recommended practice?
Answer: Use the ‘sandwich’ method.
Question: Which is NOT a listed way of ‘How Do I Put It Together?’ for communication?
Answer: Writing a report.
Question: Who else does a coach need to communicate with besides players?
Answer: Parents, Fans, and Game Officials.

M11: Planning Training Schedules

Learning Outcomes

  • Identify the key components of a well‑structured practice plan.
  • Understand the purpose of each component (warm‑up, scrimmage, etc.).
  • Grasp ‘Planning Progression’.
  • Create a simple daily practice plan.

Key Concepts

  • Practice Components: Warm‑up, Review, New Skills, Scrimmage, Team Circle/Evaluation.
  • Teaching New Skills: Keep instructions brief and focused.
  • Scrimmages: Apply drill skills in game‑like settings.
  • Planning Progression: Increase difficulty and introduce new skills according to ability level.

Practical Drills

  • 20‑Minute Mini‑Practice Plan: Design a balanced, timed practice on paper.
  • Constraint‑Led Game Design: Create a small‑sided game with a rule tweak to solve a team problem (e.g., encourage passing).

Quizzes & Answers

Question: Six key components of a well‑planned practice?
Answer: Warm‑up, Review, New Skills, Scrimmage, Team Circle, Evaluation.
Question: Main purpose of a warm‑up?
Answer: Prepare the body and reduce injury risk.
Question: Teaching new skills should…
Answer: Keep instruction to a minimum.
Question: ‘Planning Progression’ means coaches should…
Answer: Progress at a speed suitable to players’ ability.
Question: What is ‘Preseason Planning’?
Answer: Planning the program before the season begins.

M12: Introduction to Sports Science

Learning Outcomes

  • Identify the main components of physical fitness.
  • Understand the three energy systems used in basketball.
  • Learn basic principles of training (Progression, Overload).
  • Know the R.I.C.E. protocol for minor injuries.

Key Concepts

  • Components of Fitness: Cardiovascular Endurance, Muscular Strength, Flexibility, Body Composition, Speed, Agility.
  • Energy Systems:
    • Anaerobic/ATP‑PC (0–10 s): Explosive bursts.
    • Anaerobic Glycolysis (10 s – 3 min): Sustained high‑intensity efforts.
    • Aerobic (>3 min): Longer, lower‑intensity activity.
  • Principles of Training: Progression (gradually increase load); Overload (stress beyond normal capacity).
  • R.I.C.E. Protocol: Rest, Ice, Compress, Elevate.

Practical Drills

  • Lead a Dynamic Warm‑up: Guide a series of movements to prepare the body.
  • R.I.C.E. Injury Scenario: Role‑play explaining and applying R.I.C.E. to a simulated injury.

Quizzes & Answers

Question: Components of physical fitness listed?
Answer: Cardiovascular endurance, Muscular strength, Flexibility, Body composition, Speed, Agility.
Question: R.I.C.E. stands for…
Answer: Rest, Ice, Compress, Elevate.
Question: ‘Progression’ principle means…
Answer: Advancing toward a goal step by step.
Question: Primary energy system for a 0–10 s explosive move (e.g., lay‑up)?
Answer: Anaerobic/ATP‑PC.
Question: ‘Overload’ states…
Answer: The body must be stressed beyond normal capacity to improve.
